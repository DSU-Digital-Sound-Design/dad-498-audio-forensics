## Background

On November 14, 1970, Southern Airways Flight 932 — a DC-9 on charter carrying the Marshall University football team, coaches, staff, and boosters — attempted an instrument approach — a standardized landing procedure that guides pilots onto a runway using cockpit instruments and radio navigation rather than visual reference to the ground, used when visibility is too low to see the runway until the final seconds of descent — to Huntington Tri-State Airport in West Virginia. The weather was poor: ceilings ragged between 400 and 600 feet, visibility five miles, rain, fog, and smoke. The aircraft was 350 feet lower than the crew believed. It struck treetops on the west face of a hill while attempting to climb. All 75 people aboard were killed.

The cockpit voice recorder was recovered from the wreckage. It provided the only real-time record of what the crew experienced in the final minutes of flight.

> **A note on this transcript**: CVR audio from this era is federally protected and has never been publicly released. What you are reading is the NTSB's official transcript — a factual document that records words and sounds only, without interpretation. Interpreting those words and sounds is your job.

**Primary source**: Bruce Epperson, "'...6 Seconds loud, undifferentiated noise [recording ends]': A Forensic Audio Analysis of the CVR Recording of Southern Airways Flight 932," *ARSC Journal* 50, no. 1 (Spring 2019): 79. ([PDF](https://www.proquest.com/docview/2253786690?pq-origsite=gscholar&fromopenview=true&sourcetype=Scholarly%20Journals))

## Part 3: How CVR Data Corrected an Investigative Error (~15 minutes)

The unidentified sounds solved one question, but left a larger one open: *why* was the aircraft 350 feet lower than the crew believed? The NTSB initially proposed an explanation — and CVR data is what showed that explanation was wrong.

**The initial hypothesis**: Investigators considered whether the crew had been using their radar altimeters instead of their barometric altimeters. A radar altimeter measures height above the terrain directly below the aircraft. A barometric altimeter measures height above sea level, calculated from air pressure. In rugged terrain like the hills around Huntington, the two can differ substantially. If the crew relied on a barometric altimeter set for flat terrain at sea level, it could show them at 400 feet above the ground while the actual terrain had risen beneath them. This would constitute pilot error.

### Exercise 3: The Corrected Hypothesis
